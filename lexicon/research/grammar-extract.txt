1.1 Introduction
This reference grammar (still very much so a work-in-progress) contains a detailed grammatical
description of a constructed language called Classical Paternic, a language I’ve created for my own
artistic and intellectual enjoyment and as an element of my personal worldbuilding in the conworld
of N ¯ ımlad, although I hope that others will find this work both interesting and entertaining. My
goal in creating this language was to construct the outlines of a grammar and phonaesthetic that
adhered to my own sense of linguistic aesthetics. As realism is an aesthetic choice of mine and is
therefore a design constraint, I’ve largely worked to construct a language that I find linguistically
plausible and internally consistent. However, I have not hesitated to introduce grammatical or
phonological quirks that are not necessarily ’naturalistic’ for no other reason than because I wanted
to include them. Classical Paternic draws inspiration phonologically and grammatically from Latin,
Akkadian, Greenlandic, Hindustani, Nahuatl, Bengali, Hebrew, Lezgian, the Salishan languages,
Tolkien’s Sindarin and Adˆ unaic, Biblaridion’s Nekachti, and many others, though it does not greatly
resemble any of these tongues outside of the occasional lexical similarity. Please note that Classical
Paternic is an ongoing project, with no fixed endpoint. It has changed significantly in the seven or so
years I have been worked on it and will very likely change much more so long as I remain interested
in it. The description given in this document is thus not the final form of Classical Paternic, but
merely a representation of the language’s state at the time of writing.

plosives followed by a rhotic are permissable). Major phonological processes include vowel hiatus
resolution, nasal and plosive assimilation, degemination, and epenthesis to resolve illegal consonant
and vowel clusters.

2.2.2 Consonant Phonotactics and Processes
Geminated consonants are not permitted. If two identical consonants collide within a word,
they will degeminate.
An illegal consonant cluster can be broken up by inserting an epenthetical /e/ between the
two consonants of the cluster.
Alveolar fricatives cannot follow nasals. If such a cluster occurs, the preceding nasal will be
deleted and the vowel before that nasal will undergo compensatory lengthening.
2.2.3 Stress Assignment
In Classical Paternic, the stress on a word is placed on only one of two syllables: the one before
the last syllable (thepenultimatesyllable, orpenult), or the one before that (theantepenultimate
syllable, orantepenult). The final syllable is called theultima, but it is irrelevant to stress assignment.
Rule One:stress the penult if the syllable is long.
If the vowel in the penult is long or a diphthong, the syllable is long, so the stress goes on the
penult.
The penult syllable is long if it is ended by a consonant cluster. However, plosive consonants
followed by a rhotic both belong to the onset of the next syllabe, and so do not make the
penult long
Rule Two:if the penult is short, stress the antepenult.

3.1 Animacy and Number
Here a brief summary of animacy and number in Classical Patrinaic will be given.
3.1.1 Animacy
Animacy generally refers to whether a noun is living (animate) or non-living (inanimate), al-
though is no exact equivalence between a noun’s animacy and its semantic category. In the nomina-
tive case, there is no phonological pattern which can be used to discern a noun’s animacy. Animate
and inanimate nouns do, however, have differing suffixes in the other cases. Thus, whether a specific
unmarked noun is animate or inanimate is a property that must be memorized on an individual
basis.
There are roughly as many animate nouns as there are inanimate. Animacy is not restricted to
biological animacy. For example, many words referring to biologically animate things are inanimate,
such as with the wordsbilka‘plant, fungus’ andgurgael‘raven, cormorant’. Likewise, many animate
nouns, including the following, are biologically inanimate:

3.1.2 Number
In Classical Patrinaic, animate and inanimate nouns treat number differently. Animate nouns
are marked for two numbers: singular and plural. The singular refers to a single instance or unit of
something while the plural refers to several instances or a greater quantity of something. Inanimate
nouns, on the other hand, take no marking for number. The bare inanimate nounthundo‘pebble’
can refer to an individual pebble or a group of several pebbles, for instance.
Many inanimate nouns do have an inherent number, however, meaning that they are assumed
to denote a certain quantity of something. Demonstrating this, the wordghim‘teeth’ refers to a
full mouth of teeth instead of to an individual tooth. When necessary, there are methods that can
be used to explicitly mark number on inanimate nouns, however. Using the previous example, a
speaker who wished to refer to an individual tooth would quantify the word with the numeral prefix
az-‘one’. Adjectives, demonstratives, and quantifiers modifying inanimate nouns also agree with
those nouns in numbers. Thusly, speakers could refrain from quantifying inanimate nouns directly
if such a modifier would clarify. The sentences below demonstrate these two strategies:

3.2 Case Roles
Classical Patrinaic is a case-marking language, using morphology to indicate grammatical roles on
noun phrases and pronouns. There are eight cases: nominative (nom), accusative (acc), ergative
(erg), genitive (gen), allative (all), locative (loc), instrumental (ins), and comitative (com).
Almost all of the cases have multiple distinct functions, complicating the system. Therefore each
case will be accorded its own individual subsection, wherein they can be discussed in detail.
3.2.1 Nominative
The nominative case’s most common use is as the marker of the subject. It is also the case used in

Adjectives
5.1 Overview
Adjectives agree with the nouns they modify in gender, number, and case and decline accordingly.
Adjectives decline according to their declension class, which are the same as those borne by nouns.
The inanimate plural forms of adjectives will not be discussed in this document as they do not occur
in the torch and I have not the time to make the necessary charts. Adjectives typically precede
their head noun, though they can also follow them, in which case they are called postpositive
adjectives. Postpositive adjectives are typically used in place names or to put particular emphasis
on the adjective.

5.3 Degrees of Comparison
There are four degrees of comparison: positive, comparative, superlative, and equative. For any
adjective, the positive form is the bare form and conveys the basic meaning of the adjective. The
other degrees of comparison will be detailed in their own subsections below.
5.3.1 Comparative
The comparative form indicates a greater degree of an adjective, such as with the English forms
‘big’ and ‘bigger’. The comparative adjective is formed by applying the- ¯ ırsuffix to an adjective’s
stem. All comparative adjectives are of the fourth declension.
5.3.2 Superlative
The superlative form indicates the greatest degree of an adjective, such as with the English forms
‘big’ and ‘biggest’. The superlative adjective is formed by applying the-ondesuffix to an adjective’s
stem. All superlative adjectives are of the sixth declension.
5.3.3 Equative
The equative form has no morphological equivalent in English. It is used to convey that something
is at an equal level of quality, quantity, or relation. It may be translated in English using the phrases
‘as X’ or ‘equally X’, where X is the semantic meaning of the adjective. Equative adjectives are
formed by applying theibensuffix to an adjective’s stem. All equative adjectives are of the first
declension.

Finite Verbs
6.1 Overview
Verbs have two different stems each: one for perfective aspect and one for imperfective aspect
(for example,naput‘burned’ versusnembut‘was burning’). The perfective stem is more commonly
called the ‘short stem’ while the imperfective stem is referred to as the ‘long stem’. Verbs also inflect
for tense and mood in fusional suffixes, which combine with the two stems to generate conflated TAM
meanings. Additionally, verbs fall under two categories: defective and regular. Defective verbs lack
some morphological forms that regular verbs possess. Classical Paternic verbs can take one of two
voices as well, those being the active and the applicative.
6.2 Principal Parts
Both defective and regular verbs have six principal parts. The principal parts of defective verbs
are as follows: present indicative, imperfect indicative, past irrealis indicative, gnomic indicative,
preterite indicative, and the perfective participle; and the principal parts of regular verbs are: present
indiciative, imperfect indicative, past habitual indicative, gnomic indicative, preterite indicative, and
the perfective participle.

6.6 Mood
There are five moods in Classical Paternic: the indicative, the jussive, the potential, the neces-
sitative, and the old imperative
6.6.1 Jussive
All jussive mood suffixes are applied to the short stem of the verb; the long stem goes unused.
The jussive is used in order to express commands in the second person and in a hortative sense in the
first and third persons (‘let him fight’). Negated jussive verbs in present time are generally placed
in the perfect tense. The imperfect, pluperfect, and narrative tenses give a sense of obligation or
duty when combined with the jussive (‘you should have fought’).
6.6.2 Potential
The potential mood is used when expressing possibility or permission (‘can, could’ and ‘may,
might’).
6.6.3 Necessitative
The necessitative mood is used when expressing necessity or extreme obligation (‘need’ and
‘must’).

6.6.4 Old Imperative
The old imperative is an older form whose use cases have been mostly replaced by the jussive.
It is usually considered rude to use, and has a very strong force of command. The old imperative
is, however, socially acceptable to employ when the speaker has a very high level of authority over
the addressee (such as with parents to their children, or a king to his servant). It is formed simply
by placing the verb in the gnomic tense and dropping the subject pronoun.
6.7 Applicative Voice

primary object, deleting the original primary object if there was one. In Classical Paternic there

applied via prefixes, and they take the slot used by pronominal clitics. Therefore when a verb is in
the applicative voice, any pronouns in the accusative case must use their full form, as opposed to
the normal clitic form. The following table will showcase the three applicative prefixes.

Clause Structure and Syntax
9.1 Constituent Order
Classical Patrinaic is predominantly head-final in its noun phrases. Possessors and adjectives
usually precede their head noun, as can be seen by comparing the phrasekhar‘house’ with the
phrases¯ ukok khar‘the person’s house’ and¯ ukok rhus khar‘the person’s red house’. Demonstratives
and quantifiers also typically precede the noun they modify.
It should be noted, however, that the constituent orders described above are not absolute, and can
be modified with little issue. On occasion, for emphasis or poetic license, possessors and adjectives
follow the modified noun. This is a technique also regularly employed in place names. In addition,
placing a demonstrative or quantifier after its head noun can be used as a form of emphasizing
salience or definiteness.
At the clausal level, constituent order is fairly free, though there is a clear preference for verb-
final (SOV) order. Typically, clauses are made of a verb preceded by one or more case-marked
pronouns or noun phrases. The ordering of these noun phrases is determined primarily by the
discourse prominence of each constituent, which is demonstrated by the sentences below. In this
way, Classical Patrinaic operates as a scrambling language akin to Latin or Ancient Greek.

9.4 Conditional Clauses
Classical Paternic has a robust series of conditional clause types, all of which are formed by a
conditional, concessive, posterior, or similative converb in the protastis followed by a finite verb in the
apodosis. Conditional clauses begun by concessive and similative converb will not here be discussed,
nor will counterfactual conditionals. After these restrictions, there are five remaining conditional
clause types: present indicative, past indicative, future indicative, past-to-present indicative, and
past-to-future indicative.
9.4.1 General Structure
I will begin by describing the general structure of the indicative conditional clause. Conditional
clauses establish a condition, or if-clause, in the protasis, and a subsequent consequence if the
condition is fulfilled in the apodosis. The if-clause will always contain a converb and the apodosis
will always contain a finite verb.
